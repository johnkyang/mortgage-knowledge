## Definition

An **Interested Party Contribution (IPC)** is any payment made by a party with a financial interest in the real estate transaction — including the seller, builder, developer, real estate agent, or their affiliates — toward the borrower's transaction costs or other financing incentives.

IPCs are subject to strict Fannie Mae limits based on the loan-to-value ratio and property type. Contributions that exceed IPC limits must be deducted from the sales price (or appraised value, whichever is lower) before calculating the LTV.

## Context

IPCs come up frequently in underwriting when:

- The seller is paying closing costs on behalf of the buyer (seller concessions)
- The builder is offering incentives (closing cost credits, rate buydowns, upgrades)
- A real estate agent is providing a commission credit to the buyer
- An employer is assisting with closing costs for a relocated employee

The critical issue: **gifts from interested parties are not permitted.** If a gift donor has any financial stake in the transaction — even indirect — the "gift" is reclassified as an IPC and becomes subject to IPC caps, not gift fund rules. This distinction matters especially for employer gifts and builder down payment programs.

**IPC limits (subject to change — verify against current Selling Guide):**
- LTV > 90%: Maximum 3% of purchase price
- LTV 75.01%–90%: Maximum 6% of purchase price
- LTV ≤ 75%: Maximum 9% of purchase price
- Investment properties: Maximum 2% at all LTV levels
