## Summary

Social Security income (retirement, disability, SSI, survivor benefits) and other retirement income (pension, annuity) are acceptable qualifying income with documentation. A key rule: up to 15% of the total monthly Social Security payment may be treated as non-taxable and grossed up using a 25% factor — without obtaining tax returns. Full gross-up requires additional documentation. Continuance rules vary significantly by benefit type and relationship.

## Key Rules

### Social Security — Gross-Up Rule
- **15% non-taxable gross-up** (no tax return documentation required):
  - 15% of the total monthly SS payment is treated as non-taxable
  - Apply a **25% gross-up factor** to the non-taxable portion
  - Add the grossed-up amount to the total monthly payment for qualifying income
  - Example: $1,000/mo SS → $1,000 × 15% = $150 non-taxable → $150 × 25% = $37.50 → qualifying income = **$1,037.50**
- This applies to: **Social Security Retirement, Survivor Benefits, and Social Security income**
- Full gross-up of the entire monthly amount requires **additional documentation** (typically tax returns showing the non-taxable portion)
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

### Continuance
- SS income for **retirement or long-term disability** drawn from the borrower's own account/work record: No defined expiration date — **must be expected to continue**. Continuance documentation not required.
- SS income **not including retirement or long-term disability**: Requires documentation of **3-year continuance**.
- **Pending or current re-evaluation of medical eligibility** for benefits is NOT considered an indication that the income will not continue.
- **For FNMA loans**, if SS income is validated by DU Validation Service: follow DU message documentation requirements instead of standard table.
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Documentation Requirements

| Benefit Type | Drawn From | Documentation Required |
|-------------|------------|----------------------|
| Retirement | Own account/work record | SSA Award letter, or 1099, or most recent signed federal tax returns (or transcripts), or proof of current receipt. History of receipt not required. |
| Disability | Own account/work record | Same as retirement above. |
| SSI | Own account | SSA Award letter or 1099 + proof of current receipt. History not required. |
| Disability, Retirement, Survivor Benefits | Another person's account/work record | SSA Award letter or 1099 + proof of current receipt + **3-year continuance** (e.g., verification of beneficiary's age) |
| Borrower will begin receiving before first mortgage payment date | Any | SSA Award Letter required. Proof of current receipt not required. |

(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## VA Benefits

- Document receipt with a letter or distribution form from the VA
- Verify continuance for **minimum 3 years** from mortgage application date
- Exception: VA **retirement or long-term disability** benefits do not require continuance verification
- **Educational benefits** (e.g., G.I. Bill) are **not** acceptable income
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Exceptions & Nuances

- **Short-term disability** (non-SS) — covered under [[temporary-leave-income]], not this section
- **Long-term disability (non-SS)** — referenced in the Temporary Leave section of the guide but governed by different rules than SS disability
- **Continuance bifurcation**: SS retirement/LTD from own record = no continuance docs needed; SS from another person's record = 3-year continuance required. This is a commonly missed distinction.

## Common Pitfalls

- Applying the full gross-up (100% of SS as non-taxable) without supporting tax returns — only 15% can be treated as non-taxable without docs
- Forgetting to document 3-year continuance for SS drawn from another person's account (survivor benefits, disability on another's work record)
- Treating SSI the same as retirement SS — SSI from own account still requires proof of current receipt (different from retirement)
- Using G.I. Bill educational benefits as qualifying income — explicitly ineligible
