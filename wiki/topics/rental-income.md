## Summary

Rental income is acceptable qualifying income when it can be established as stable and likely to continue. Income is calculated either from Schedule E (tax returns) or from lease agreements (when tax return history is absent or inadequate). The calculation method, eligible properties, and documentation requirements differ significantly between subject property and non-subject property, and between FNMA and FHLMC.

## Key Rules

- Rental income is eligible from: 2–4-unit primary residences (borrower occupies one unit), and 1–4-unit investment properties. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])
- Rental income from a **1-unit primary residence or second home** generally cannot be used to qualify, except for [[boarder-income]] or programs like HomeReady/Home Possible. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])
- Rental income from **non-subject properties** has no property type restriction — commercial rental income is acceptable if documented per guidelines. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])
- Rental income from properties reported through a partnership or S corporation must be analyzed as **self-employment income**. See [[self-employed-income]]. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])
- Rental income does **not** require 3-year continuance documentation — it is on the indefinite-continuance list. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Net Rental Income Calculation

### When Using a Lease (75% Rule)
- Qualifying rental income = **75% of gross monthly rent** (per lease or Form 1007/1025 market rent)
- The 25% adjustment covers vacancies, maintenance, and unexpected expenses
- Required form: FNMA Form 1007 (single unit) or 1025 (2–4 units); FHLMC Form 72 or 1000
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

### When Using Schedule E
Add back to the reported net income:
- Insurance
- Mortgage interest
- Real estate taxes
- Depreciation/depletion
- HOA dues
- One-time losses
- Non-cash deductions

**Note**: Insurance, mortgage interest, taxes, and HOA dues can only be added back if they are already included in the PITIA payment being used in the DTI calculation. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

**Averaging:**
- Property in service for the **full year**: average over 12 months
- Property in service for **less than the full year**: average over the number of months in service
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Treatment of Rental Income (or Loss) in DTI

### Primary Residence Rental Units (2–4 unit, borrower occupies)
- Net rental income is **added** to total monthly income
- Full PITIA of the property is counted as a monthly obligation
- Income is NOT netted against the PITIA

### Non-Primary Residence Rental (Investment property, non-subject)
- If net rental income **minus** full PITIA is **positive** → add the net to monthly income
- If net rental income **minus** full PITIA is **negative** → add the net loss to monthly obligations
- Full PITIA is factored into the net rental income/loss calculation — do NOT also count PITIA as a separate obligation
- Borrower's primary residence PITIA (or rent) is always counted separately as a monthly obligation
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## FNMA — Borrower History Requirements

| Borrower Situation | Rental Income Restriction |
|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Owns primary residence (or has housing expense) + 1+ year rental history or property management experience | No restriction on income amount |
| Owns primary residence + less than 1 year rental history — subject primary residence | Income cannot exceed PITIA of subject property |
| Owns primary residence + less than 1 year rental history — subject investment property | Income can only offset PITIA |
| Owns primary residence + less than 1 year rental history — non-subject new rental | Same restrictions as above by property type |
| Does NOT own a primary residence and has no housing expense | Rental income from subject or non-subject property **cannot** be used |

(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

**FHLMC exception**: Freddie Mac does NOT require the borrower to currently own a primary residence to use rental income when purchasing a 2–4 unit primary residence. FHLMC does not consider a 2–4 unit primary residence a rental property. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Documenting Subject Property Rental Income (FNMA)

| History of Rental? | Transaction | Documentation Required |
|-------------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Yes | Refinance | Form 1007 or 1025 + most recent year Schedule E (or current lease if exception applies) |
| No | Purchase | Form 1007 or 1025 + current executed lease agreements |
| No | Refinance | Form 1007 or 1025 + current executed lease agreements |

(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## When a Lease May Replace Schedule E

A lease agreement may be used when:
- Property was acquired during or after the last tax filing year
- Property experienced significant rental interruptions (e.g., major renovation)
- Property is being placed in service in the current calendar year (departing residence conversion)
- Underwriter documents another qualifying exception with written explanation

**Lease must be supported by:**
- Form 1007/1025 (FNMA) or Form 72/1000 (FHLMC) confirming market rent, OR
- Two consecutive months of rental payment receipts, OR
- Security deposit + first month's rent (for newly executed leases)
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Departing Residence — Converting Primary to Rental

When a borrower converts their primary residence to a rental property:
- Rental Income Requirements apply plus reserve and multiple financed properties requirements
- Documentation: Executed lease + one of the following:
  - Form 1007/1025 supporting the lease amount, OR
  - Two consecutive months of rental payments received, OR
  - Security deposit + first month's rent for newly executed leases
- FHLMC: Net rental income from a departing residence can only **offset** the PITIA (plus MI, HOA, and secondary financing) unless the borrower has documented 1+ year of investment property management experience — in which case full net income may be used
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## ADU Rental Income (Subject 1-Unit Primary)

**FNMA:**
- Eligible only for 1-unit primary residence, purchase or limited cash-out refi
- Only one ADU's rental income allowed
- Qualifying rental income capped at **30% of total qualifying income**
- All standard rental documentation requirements apply

**FHLMC:**
- Purchase or no cash-out refi only
- Requires current, fully executed lease
- Two months' rental payment receipts (or security deposit + first month for new leases)
- Appraisal must include ADU condition, SF, bed/bath count, zoning confirmation, at least one comp sale with ADU, and rental analysis with minimum 3 comparable rentals (at least one must be a rented ADU)
- Landlord education required for purchase transactions unless borrower has 1+ year investment property management experience
(Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Exceptions & Nuances

- **Partial or no Schedule E history**: When property was out of service for a period (renovation), the UW may annualize the partial-year income from Schedule E or use a lease. Document the renovation with repair costs and reduced Fair Rental Days on Schedule E.
- **Stability analysis**: UW must review Fair Rental Days on Schedule E year-over-year. Significant volatility requires additional documentation and analysis.
- **Non-subject commercial property**: Commercial rental income owned by the borrower is acceptable if documented per standard rental income requirements — no property type restriction on non-subject properties.
- **Gross rent reporting (not used to qualify)**: Even when rental income is not used to qualify, gross monthly rent on the subject property must still be documented for reporting purposes.

## Common Pitfalls

- Using rental income from a 1-unit primary residence or second home without HomeReady/Home Possible or boarder income exception
- Failing to confirm borrower has housing expense when purchasing an investment property (FNMA requires primary residence or housing expense to use investment property rental income)
- Using the 75% lease method without the required Form 1007/1025 supporting market rent
- Adding back PITIA components to Schedule E that are not included in the payment used in DTI
- Counting the PITIA as a separate monthly obligation when it's already netted into the rental income/loss for non-primary rental properties
- Not recognizing that partnership/S-corp rental income flows through SE income analysis

## Interpretation Notes

> ⚠️ **Interpretation**: Content below is not directly sourced. It reflects practical application or professional judgment.

- When determining whether a borrower has "property management experience" to unlock full rental income (vs. PITIA-offset only), a signed Schedule E showing 365 Fair Rental Days for any rental property is the cleanest documentation. CPA letters alone are generally not sufficient.
- For departing residences, some underwriters require evidence the tenant has physically taken possession before closing to accept the lease-based income. Loanepot's guide allows security deposit + first month's rent as an alternative — verify with lender policy on specific files.
