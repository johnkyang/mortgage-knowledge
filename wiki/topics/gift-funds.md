## Summary

Gift funds are a permitted source of funds for down payment, closing costs, and reserves on Fannie Mae conventional loans, subject to occupancy type and loan-to-value restrictions. Gifts must be genuine transfers — not loans in disguise — and require specific documentation to verify the donor relationship, the source of the gift, and the transfer of funds.

## Key Rules

- Gift funds are eligible from family members, domestic partners, fiancés/fiancées, and other eligible donors as defined by Fannie Mae. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- For **principal residence** and **second home** loans, gift funds may be used for the entire down payment with no minimum borrower contribution required — provided DU does not require reserves. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- For **investment properties**, gifts are **not permitted** as a source of funds. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- When the LTV is greater than 80% on a principal residence **and** the loan is a purchase transaction, the entire down payment may come from gift funds. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- When the LTV is 80% or below on a principal residence or second home, at least 5% of the down payment must come from the borrower's own funds if the purchase price exceeds the conforming loan limit — this rule is DU-determined and may vary. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- The gift must not require repayment. A promissory note, lien, or other repayment obligation disqualifies the funds as a gift. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- A gift letter is required, signed by the donor, stating: the dollar amount, the date of the gift, the donor's name, address, phone, relationship to borrower, and that no repayment is required. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- Gift funds must be documented to show the donor's ability to give (bank statement showing sufficient funds prior to transfer) and evidence of receipt (borrower's bank statement showing deposit). (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])
- If the gift funds are deposited before the loan closes, the funds must be documented on the borrower's bank statement. If not yet deposited, a certified check or wire transfer documentation may be used. (Source: [[source-selling-guide-b3-4.3-04]])

## Documentation Requirements

See [[checklist-gift-funds-docs]] for the complete documentation checklist.

**Core requirements:**
- Signed gift letter with all required elements
- Donor's bank statement (most recent 2 months) showing ability to provide the gift
- Evidence of transfer (wire confirmation, certified check, or borrower bank statement showing deposit)
- If deposited: borrower's bank statement showing the deposit amount matching gift letter

## Exceptions & Nuances

- **Employer gifts**: Gifts from employers are treated differently — they may be characterized as [[def-interested-party-contribution|interested party contributions]] if the employer has a financial interest in the transaction. Verify carefully.
- **Gift of equity**: A gift of equity from a family member seller is a separate scenario covered in [[gift-of-equity]]. It is documented differently (gift letter plus HUD-1/CD showing equity credit, not a cash transfer).
- **Down payment assistance programs**: Grants from government or non-profit entities are not the same as gifts and are covered under [[grants-dpa]].
- **Gift funds as reserves**: Gift funds may be acceptable for reserves only if the donor is a family member and the funds are in the borrower's account. See [[gift-funds-for-reserves]] and [[reserve-requirements]].
- **Large deposits**: If gift funds appear as a large deposit in the borrower's bank statements, additional sourcing documentation will be required. See [[large-deposits]] and [[scenario-gift-large-deposit]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Accepting a gift letter that doesn't state "no repayment required" explicitly
- Not obtaining donor's bank statement showing funds available before transfer
- Treating a personal loan from a family member as a gift — must verify no obligation exists
- Confusing employer down payment assistance with a gift (IPC rules may apply)
- Missing the investment property prohibition — gifts are never allowed for investment transactions
- Failing to re-verify gift receipt if closing is delayed after gift letter was obtained

## Interpretation Notes

> ⚠️ **Interpretation**: Content below is not directly sourced. It reflects practical application or professional judgment.

- In practice, underwriters often require 60 days of donor bank statements when the gift represents a large portion of total assets, even when the Selling Guide only requires documentation of the most recent statement. This is a common lender overlay, not a Fannie Mae requirement.
- When gift funds are wired directly to closing, some lenders accept the HUD-1/CD as evidence of receipt without a separate bank statement showing the deposit. Confirm with lender policy.
