## Summary

Cryptocurrency (e.g., Bitcoin) is considered property by the IRS — not U.S. currency — and cannot be documented under standard FNMA/FHLMC asset guidelines. It must be converted to U.S. dollars and deposited into an eligible financial institution account before it can be used for down payment, closing costs, or reserves.

## Key Rules

- Cryptocurrency ownership proof requires a digital process that does not conform to FNMA/FHLMC asset statement documentation standards. As a result, crypto held in its original form **does not satisfy asset verification guidelines.** (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])
- Cryptocurrency **must be converted to U.S. dollars** and deposited into an eligible asset account (U.S. or state-regulated financial institution) before it can be considered eligible funds. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

### Documentation Requirements (After Conversion)

All of the following must be satisfied:
- Documented evidence that the virtual currency was exchanged into U.S. dollars and is held in a U.S. or state-regulated financial institution.
- Funds verified in U.S. dollars **prior to closing** the loan.
- If the converted funds appear as a large deposit: the company must obtain sufficient documentation to verify the funds originated from the borrower's virtual currency account. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

### Crypto-Secured Debt

Monthly debt payments secured by cryptocurrency must be **included in the borrower's DTI** and are treated as installment debts secured by a financial asset. (Source: [[source-loandepot-conventional-guide-2026-03]])

## Exceptions & Nuances

- Even after conversion, if the deposit is large (>50% of monthly qualifying income), large deposit sourcing rules apply. Crypto origin is an acceptable source if properly documented.
- The "eligible asset account" requirement means funds must clear into a checking, savings, or similar account — not remain in a crypto exchange wallet.
